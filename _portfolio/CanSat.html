CanSat Project Overview

The CanSat 2021 project involved designing, simulating, and testing a CanSat that featured a container and two auto-rotating maple seed payloads. Our team, Sammard (#1966), successfully achieved the mission objectives by combining innovative mechanical, electrical, and software design principles. The project aimed to demonstrate robust engineering solutions for a challenging aerospace task.

Mission Objectives

Primary Objectives

  • Design a CanSat with a container and two science payloads that mimic the behavior of auto-rotating maple seeds.
  • Ensure the CanSat descends at a controlled rate of 15 m/s ± 5 m/s.
  • Deploy the payloads sequentially at altitudes of 500 m and 400 m.
  • Transmit telemetry data from both the container and the payloads to the ground station.

Secondary Objectives

  • Educate the public about aerospace engineering through outreach activities.
  • Foster teamwork and collaboration among team members.

Constraints

  • Total mass: 600 ± 10 grams.
  • Survive 15 Gs of launch acceleration and 30 Gs of shock.

System Overview

  • Container: A cylindrical structure designed to house and deploy the science payloads. Equipped with a parachute for controlled descent and telemetry systems for data relay.
  • Payloads: Two auto-rotating maple seed-inspired designs equipped with sensors for measuring altitude, temperature, and rotation rate.

Mechanical Design

Container

  • Constructed using 3D-printed ABS material for lightweight durability.
  • Carbon fiber spars provided structural integrity.
  • Integrated cavities for servo-driven payload release mechanisms.
  • Parachute attachment point and aerodynamic design ensured stable descent.

Payloads

  • Wings optimized using Blade Element Momentum Theory for efficient drag and lift balance.
  • Constructed with NACA 4415 airfoil cross-sections for optimal aerodynamic performance.
  • Designed for passive auto-rotation upon release.

Electronics and Sensors

Container Sensors

  • GPS (Ublox NEO-M8N): Real-time position tracking.
  • Pressure Sensor (MS5611): Altitude calculation.
  • Battery voltage monitoring.

Payload Sensors

  • Pressure and temperature sensors.
  • BNO080 gyroscope for rotation rate measurement.

Telemetry

  • Data transmitted via XBee S2C modules to the ground station.

Software Design

  • Telemetry: Real-time data transmission at 1 Hz. Data storage in flash memory for redundancy.
  • Control: Servo actuation for sequential payload deployment. Simulation mode for pre-launch testing.
  • Ground Station: Custom GUI for live telemetry display and data logging.

Descent Control

Container

  • Parachute with a dome shape and a 5% spill hole ensured stable descent at ~15 m/s.

Payloads

  • Spin-induced passive descent achieved using optimized maple seed-inspired wings.
  • Terminal velocity of ~4.46 m/s ensured controlled descent and safe landing.

Key Achievements

  • Successfully tested payload release mechanisms and descent control systems.
  • Developed a robust telemetry system for seamless communication with the ground station.
  • Demonstrated innovative use of CAD, CFD, and simulation tools in the design process.
  • Delivered the CanSat within weight and budget constraints despite pandemic-related challenges.

Challenges and Solutions

  • Challenge: Limited resources and laboratory access during the pandemic.
    Solution: Increased reliance on simulation tools and virtual collaboration.
  • Challenge: High descent velocity of payloads in initial designs.
    Solution: Optimized wing planform area and airfoil design.

Impact and Legacy

This project highlights the team’s ability to integrate multidisciplinary engineering principles into a complex aerospace challenge. It also underscores the importance of collaboration, adaptability, and innovation in achieving ambitious goals under constraints.
